SOLAR SYSTEM — QUICK FACTS

The Sun
The Sun contains about 99.8% of the total mass of the Solar System. It is roughly
4.6 billion years old and is classified as a G-type main-sequence star (G2V).

Mercury
Mercury is the smallest planet and the closest to the Sun. It has no moons and almost
no atmosphere. A single day on Mercury (sunrise to sunrise) lasts about 176 Earth days.

Earth
Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known planet to support life.
About 71% of its surface is covered by water. It has one natural satellite, the Moon,
which is roughly 384,400 km away on average.

Mars
Mars is known as the Red Planet because of iron oxide on its surface. It has two small
moons, Phobos and Deimos, and is home to Olympus Mons, the tallest volcano in the
Solar System at about 22 km high.

Jupiter
Jupiter is the largest planet, with a mass more than twice that of all the other
planets combined. Its Great Red Spot is a giant storm that has raged for centuries.
Jupiter has at least 95 known moons, the four largest being Io, Europa, Ganymede,
and Callisto.

Saturn
Saturn is famous for its prominent ring system, made mostly of ice and rock. It is the
least dense planet in the Solar System — less dense than water.

Neptune
Neptune is the farthest planet from the Sun and the windiest, with winds reaching up
to about 2,100 km/h. It was the first planet located through mathematical prediction
rather than direct observation, in 1846.
